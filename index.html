We are the undeniably beautiful result of an evolutionary fuzzing algorithm running for a few billion years on earth. How difficult could it be to use fuzzing and find bugs in the software we, most recently, created ourselves? Especially when we have powerful operating systems, program analysis, and machine learning? In other words, I am broadly interested in operating systems, program analysis for software testing, and applications of machine learning to system problems.

Past Experience

I was a postdoctoral researcher at the Computer Science department of Brown University working under the mentorship of Assistant Professor Vasileios Kemerlis between 2021-23. In 2020, I received my PhD from Columbia University, under the supervision of Roxana Geambasu. Over my years in Columbia, I collaborated with various great people, including Jason Nieh, Baishakhi Ray, Suman Jana, Daniel Hsu, and, of course, my primary advisor Roxana Geambasu. Moreover, during the summers of 2017 and 2018, I had two wonderful internships at Microsoft Research, where, along with Marina Polishchuk and Patrice Godefroid, I did work on Cloud Service API Testing. Here is my research statement and my dissertation as well as a literature review in the area of software testing which was part of my candidacy exam.

Before joining Columbia, I was a member of the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN), where I contributed to Agile Infrastructure. Some distinguished former colleagues made these interesting presentations: Configuration Management and CERN's OpenStack Cloud. My alma mater is the National and Kapodistrian University of Athens (UOA), Department of Informatics and Telecommunications. I worked there with Alex Delis from Management of Data, Information & Knowledge group and with Mema Roussopoulos from the Distributed Systems Lab.
